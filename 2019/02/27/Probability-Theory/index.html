What is it?

What on earth is a Norm? A ‘normal’ number? Well, NO!

Treat it as some kind of ‘distance’ function to 0\bold{0}0. In machine learning, we usually measure the size of a vector (distance between the vector and 0\bold{0}0) using function called a norm. It looks like this:

L1L^1L1 norm

In several machine learning applications, it is important decriminate between elements that are exactly zero and elements that are very small and close to zero. In these cases, we turn to a function that grows at the same rate in call locations, but retains mathematical simplicity: the L1​L^1​L1​ norm.

\begin{equation} ||x||_1 = \displaystyle \sum_i|x_i|\end{equation}\tag{2}

The L1​L^1​L1​ is commonly used in machine learning when the difference between zero and nonzero elements is very important.

Max norm

Another norm that commonly arises in machine learning applications is the L∞​L^{\infty}​L∞​, A.K.A the max norm. This norm simplies to the absolute value of the elements with largest magnitude in the vector:

\begin{equation} ||x||_\infty = \displaystyle \text{max}_i|x_i| \end{equation}\tag{3}​

Frobenius norm

Sometimes, we may also want to evalutate the size of a matrix. In the context of deep learning, the most common way is using the obscure Frobenius norm:

\begin{equation} ||A||_F = \displaystyle \sqrt {\sum_{i,j} A_{i,j}^2}\end{equation} \tag{4}

which is anologous to the Euclidean norm.

Probability and Information Theory

Three possible source of uncertainty:

  • Inherent stochasticity in the system being modeled. For example, we can crate a theoretical scenarios that we postulate to have random dynamics, such as a hypothetical card game, where we assume the cards are truly shuffled into a random order.
  • Incomplete observations. Even deterministic systems can appear stochastic when we cannot observe all variables that drive the behaviour of the system.
  • Incomlete modeling. When we use a model that must discard some of the information we have observed, the discarded information might result in uncertainty in the model’s predictions.

In many cases, it is more practical to use a simple but uncertain rule, rather than a complex but certain one.

Frequentist probability V.S. Bayesian probability

The frequentist sees the probability as the long-run expected frequency of occurrence. P(A)=n/NP(A) = n/NP(A)=n/N, where nnn is the number of times even AAA occurs in the NNN opportunities.

The Bayesian view of probability is related to degree of belief. It is a measure of the plausiblity of an event given imcomplete knowledge.

Gaussian Distribution

The most commonly used distribution over real numbers is the normal distribution, also known as Gaussian distribution:

\begin{equation} \mathcal{N}(x;\mu, \sigma^2) = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} } \text{exp} \left( - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x-\mu)^2\right) \end{equation} \tag{5}​

The two parameters μ∈R​\mu \in \mathbb{R}​μ∈R​ and σ∈(0,∞)​\sigma \in (0, \infty)​σ∈(0,∞)​ control the normal distribution. The parameter μ​\mu​μ​ gives the corrodinate of the central peak. This is also the mean of the distribution: E[x]=μ​\mathbb{E}[\text{x}] = \mu​E[x]=μ​. The standard deviation of the distribution is given by the σ​\sigma​σ​, and the variance by σ2​\sigma^2​σ2​.

Many distributions we wish to model are truly close to being normal distributions. The central limit theorem shows that the sum of many independent random variables is approximately normally distributed.

The normal distribution encodes the maximum amount of the uncertainty over the real numbers.

The normal distribution generalizes to Rn​\mathbb{R}^n​Rn​, in which case it is knonw as the multivariate normal distribution. It may parametrized with a positive definite (with all positive λ​\lambda​λ​, A.K.A, eigenvalues) symmetric matrix Σ​\boldsymbol{\Sigma}​Σ​:

\begin{equation} \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}; \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{(2\pi)^n \text{det}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma)}}} \text{exp} \left( - \frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}( \boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right) \end{equation} \tag{6}

The parameter μ​\boldsymbol{\mu}​μ​ still gives the mean of the distribution, even it’s now vectorised. The parameter Σ​\boldsymbol{\Sigma}​Σ​ gives the covariance matrix of the distribution. As in invariance case, when we wish to evaluate the PDF (probability density function) several times for many different values of the parameters, the covariance is not a computationally efficient way to parametrize the distribution, since we need to invert Σ​\boldsymbol{\Sigma}​Σ​ to evaluate the PDF. We can instead use a **precise matrix β​\boldsymbol{\beta}​β​:

$ \mathcal{N} (\boldsymbol{x}; \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\beta^{-1}}) = \sqrt{\frac{\text{det}{\boldsymbol{\beta}}}{(2\pi)^n}} \text{exp} \left(-\frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^{\top} \boldsymbol{\beta}(\boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \right)$​

Exponential and Laplace Distributions

In deep learning, we often want to have a probablity distribution with a sharp point at x=0x=0x=0. To accomplish this, we can use the exponentional distribution.

\begin{equation} p(x;\lambda) = \lambda \boldsymbol{1}_{x \geq 0} \text{exp}(-\lambda x) \end{equation} \tag{8}​

The exponentional distribution uses the indicator function 1x≥0\boldsymbol{1}_{x \geq 0}1x≥0​ to assign the probability zero to all negative values of xxx.

A close related distribution that allows us to place a sharp peak of probability mass at an arbitrary point μ\muμ is the Laplace distribution.

\begin{equation} \text{Laplace}(x;\mu, \gamma) = \frac{1}{2 \gamma} \text{exp} \left( - \frac{|x-\mu|}{\gamma} \right) \end{equation} \tag{9}

The Dirac delta function is defined such that it is zero-valued everywhere except 0, yet integers to 1.

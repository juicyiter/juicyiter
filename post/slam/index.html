Homography and Fundamental matrix

\(\boldsymbol{H} = \boldsymbol{K} (\boldsymbol{R} - \frac{\boldsymbol{tn}^\top}{d}) \boldsymbol{K}^{-1}, \, \boldsymbol{F} = \boldsymbol{K} \boldsymbol{E} \boldsymbol{K}^{-1} = \boldsymbol{K} \boldsymbol{t}^{\wedge} \boldsymbol{R}\boldsymbol{K}^{-1}\)

  • Similarities

    • They all relates to the transformation between to image points.
    • Both of them are used in epipolar geometry to solve \(\boldsymbol{R}\) and \(\boldsymbol{t}\) .
    • Both of them are \(3 \times 3\) matrices
    • Both of them can be solved using eight-point-algorithm

  • Differences

    • \(\boldsymbol{H}\) describes the transformation between points of two images that lies on the same plane, while \(\boldsymbol{F}\) describes the transformation between points of two images that lies on the different (or same) plane.
    • \(\boldsymbol{H}\) with \(8\) DOF, while \(\boldsymbol{F}\) with \(5\) DOF
    • Solving \(\boldsymbol{H}\) at least needs \(\boldsymbol{4}\) pairs of points while \(\boldsymbol{F}\) acquires at least \(\boldsymbol{8}\) pairs.

When the landmarks (feature points) are co-planar or the camera only rotates, fundamental matrix degenerates to homography. Now if carrying on using eight-point-algorithm to solve \(\boldsymbol{F}\), “extra DOF” of \(\boldsymbol{F}\) will be mainly determined by noises. Empirically, we estimate both \(\boldsymbol{F}\) and \(\boldsymbol{H}\), and choose the one with smaller re-projection error.

A homography is a special case of the Fundamental matrix.4 For a general case of a scene with objects with different depth, the geometry between two views is encapsulated with \(\boldsymbol{F}\), which is a \(3 \times 3\) matrix of rank \(2\) . Say \(X\) in the scene corresponds to \(x\) in one view and \(x^{\prime}\) in another. The relation between them is \({x^\prime}^\top \boldsymbol{F} x = 0\) .If we know the position of \(x\) in one image, then the search of \(x^\prime\) in another image is reduced to the search on the epipolar line \(l = \boldsymbol{F} x\) .

A planar homography \(\boldsymbol{H}\) satisfies the constrains \(x^\prime = \boldsymbol{H} x\) . \(\boldsymbol{H}\) is a a \(3 \times 3\) matrix of rank \(3\) in which there is one-to-one point correspondence between the first and second image.

TODO Optimisation Methods

Problem:

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{x}^* &= \arg \min_{\boldsymbol{x}} F(\boldsymbol{x})\\
&= \arg \min_{\boldsymbol{x}} \frac{1}{2} || f(\boldsymbol{x}) ||^2\\
&= \arg \min_{\boldsymbol{x}} \frac{1}{2} || \boldsymbol{y} - \hat{\boldsymbol{y}} ||^2 \end{aligned} \end{equation}

  • Gaussian Newton

    Estimate \(\red{f}(\boldsymbol{x} + \Delta{\boldsymbol{x}})\) using first-order Taylor expansion.

    \begin{equation} f(\boldsymbol{x} + \Delta \boldsymbol{x}) = f(\boldsymbol{x}) + \boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})^\top \Delta \boldsymbol{x} \end{equation}

    Then solve

    \begin{equation} \min_{\Delta \boldsymbol{x}} \frac{1}{2} || f(\boldsymbol{x}) + \boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})^\top \Delta \boldsymbol{x} ||^2 \end{equation}

    for optimal \(\Delta \boldsymbol{x}^*\).

    \begin{equation} \underbrace{\boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x}) \boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})^\top}_{\boldsymbol{H}(\boldsymbol{x})} \Delta \boldsymbol{x} = \underbrace{-\boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})f(\boldsymbol{x})}_{\boldsymbol{g}} \end{equation}

    Cons:

    • Without good stability, may not converge if \(\boldsymbol{H}\) is singular.
    • If the step \(\Delta \boldsymbol{x}\) is too large, then the assumption of Taylor expansion fails to stand.

  • Levenberg Marquadt

    Constrain equation (3) in \(|| \boldsymbol{D} \Delta \boldsymbol{x} ||^2 \leq \mu\) .

    Solve Lagrangian equation

    \begin{equation} \mathcal{L}(\Delta \boldsymbol{x}, \lambda) = \frac{1}{2} || f(\boldsymbol{x}) + \boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})^\top \Delta \boldsymbol{x} ||^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} (|| \boldsymbol{D} \Delta \boldsymbol{x} ||^2 - \mu) \end{equation}

    for optimal \(\Delta \boldsymbol{x}^*\).

    \begin{equation} (\boldsymbol{H} + \lambda \boldsymbol{D}^\top \boldsymbol{D}) \Delta \boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{g}. \end{equation}

    Simplest cast is when \(\boldsymbol{D} = \boldsymbol{I}\) .

    while iterating, another term, called gain ratio is calculated,

    \begin{equation} \mathcal{g} = \frac{f(\boldsymbol{x} + \Delta \boldsymbol{x}) - f(\boldsymbol{x})}{\boldsymbol{J}(\boldsymbol{x})^\top \Delta \boldsymbol{x}} \end{equation}

    • if \(\mathcal{g} < 0.25\), then in next iteration \(\mu = 0.5 \mu\). (estimated decrement is too large)
    • if \(\mathcal{g} > 0.75\), then \(\mu = 2 \mu\). (estimated is nearly the actual, let’s try larger radius)
    • if \(\mathcal{g}\) is greater than some threshold, then this \(\Delta \boldsymbol{x}\) is presumed good, \(\boldsymbol{x}_{k+1} = \boldsymbol{x}_k + \Delta \boldsymbol{x}\).

    \(\boldsymbol{D}\) here can make the constraint ball become ellipsoid, which will be larger constraints on smaller gradient. Empirically, \(\boldsymbol{D}\) is set to non-negative diagonal matrix, which typically using square root of the diagonals of \(\boldsymbol{J}^\top \boldsymbol{J}\).

    In equation (6), when \(\lambda\) is small, \(\boldsymbol{H}\) dominants, LM behaves more like GN.(final stage) When \(\lambda\) is large, \(\lambda \boldsymbol{D}^\top \boldsymbol{D}\) dominants, LM behaves more like gradient descent.(initial stage)

    Compare to GN, LM, to some extent, rectifies GN’s problems and is more robust, but with slower converging speed, which is why LM is also called Damped Newton Method.

Kalman Filter 7

Consider the transition model and sensor model for a simple linear system:

\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \boldsymbol{x}_k = \boldsymbol{A}_k \boldsymbol{x}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{u}_k + \boldsymbol{w}_k \\
\boldsymbol{z}_k = \boldsymbol{C}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k + \boldsymbol{v}_k \end{cases} \quad k = 1, \cdots, N \end{equation}

where

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{w}_k \sim N(0, \boldsymbol{R}_k) \\
\boldsymbol{v}_k \sim N(0, \boldsymbol{Q}_k). \end{equation}

The goal is to estimate \(\boldsymbol{x}_k\)’s distribution which is also gaussian distributed. From Bayesian rules, we have

\begin{equation} P(\boldsymbol{x}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k}) = \frac{P(\boldsymbol{z}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_{k}, \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k-1}) P(\boldsymbol{x}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k-1})}{P(\boldsymbol{z}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k-1})} = \frac{P(\boldsymbol{z}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_{k}) P(\boldsymbol{x}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k-1})}{P(\boldsymbol{z}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k-1})} \end{equation}

Say we now know the posterior \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1}\) and the covariance matrix \(\hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1}\) of \(\boldsymbol{x}\) at time \(k-1\). We want to know the posterior \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}\) and the covariance \(\hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}\) at time \(k\).

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &N(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}, \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}) \\
&=P(\boldsymbol{x}_k | \boldsymbol{x}_0, \boldsymbol{u}_{1:k}, \boldsymbol{z}_{1:k}) \\
&=\eta N(\boldsymbol{C}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k, \boldsymbol{Q}_k) \cdot N(\boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{u}_k, \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}_k^\top + \boldsymbol{R}_k) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Here, \(N(\boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{u}_k, \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1} \boldsymbol{A}_k^\top + \boldsymbol{R}_k)\) is the prior probability of \(x_k\). Let

\begin{equation} \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k} = \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{u}_k \\
\check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} = \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}_k^\top + \boldsymbol{R}_k) \end{equation}

so equation (11) becomes

\begin{aligned} N(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}, \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}) = \eta N(\boldsymbol{C}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k, \boldsymbol{Q}_k) \cdot N(\check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}, \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}). \end{aligned}

Since we already know that both sides of the equation are gaussian distributed, so we can compare the quadratic form of the \(\exp\) part to derive the \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}, \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}\).

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} &(\boldsymbol{x}_k - \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k})^\top \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{x}_k - \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}) \\
& \, \dot{=} \, (\boldsymbol{z}_k - \boldsymbol{C}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k)^\top \boldsymbol{Q}_k^{-1} (\boldsymbol{z}_k - \boldsymbol{C}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k)\\
&+ (\boldsymbol{x}_k - \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k})^\top \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} (\boldsymbol{x}_k - \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}) \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Compare the second-order coefficients, we have

\begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} \dot{=} \boldsymbol{C}_k^\top \boldsymbol{Q}_k^{-1}\boldsymbol{C}_k + \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} \end{equation}

Compare the first order coefficients, we have

\begin{equation} \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k} = \boldsymbol{C}_k^\top \boldsymbol{Q}_k^{-1}\boldsymbol{z}_k + \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}^{-1} \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k} \end{equation}

Using SMW we can derive \(\hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k}\) from (14).

Then \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}\) from (15).

All of this can be summarised as two steps:

  • Prediction

    \[ \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k} = \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{u}_k \]

    \[ \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} = \boldsymbol{A}_k \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1}^{-1} \boldsymbol{A}_k^\top + \boldsymbol{R}_k \]

  • Update: calculate \(\boldsymbol{K}\) first, which is also called Kalman gain

    \[ \boldsymbol{K} = \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} \boldsymbol{C}_k^\top (\boldsymbol{C}_k \check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} \boldsymbol{C}_k^\top)^{-1} \]

    then the posterior probability distribution.

    \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_k = \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_k + \boldsymbol{K}(\boldsymbol{z}_k - \boldsymbol{C}_k \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_k) \]

    \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} = (\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{KC}_k)\check{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k} \]

For non-linear system, use first order Taylor expansion at \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1}\) and \(\hat{\boldsymbol{P}}_{k-1}\),

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{x}_k \approx f(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1}, \boldsymbol{u}_k) + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}_{k-1}} \Bigg |_{\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1}} (\boldsymbol{x}_{k-1} - \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k-1}) + \boldsymbol{w}_k \end{equation}

\begin{equation} \boldsymbol{z}_k \approx h(\check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}) + \frac{\partial h}{\partial \boldsymbol{x}_{k}} \Bigg |_{\check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}} (\boldsymbol{x}_k - \check{\boldsymbol{x}}_{k}) + \boldsymbol{v}_k \end{equation}

Then using linear system comprehension.
